        \textbf{Abstract:} We investigate whether standard neural networks (MLP with GELU
        activations) trained on physics problems exhibit \emph{RG-like behavior},
        using the 2D Ising block-spin transformation as a controlled test.
        The main positive finding is that MLPs trained with block-spin labels
        achieve test MSE $1.26 \pm 0.65$, notably lower than the random-label baseline
        ($1.81 \pm 0.02$, $n=10$ seeds; $95\%$ bootstrap CI for the mean difference
        $[0.31, 0.79]$ excludes zero, satisfying Criterion R1).
        The main negative finding is that a single linear layer achieves
        $0.20 \pm 0.11$, substantially outperforming the MLP ($1.26 \pm 0.65$)

        This demonstrates that the block-spin target at $L=16$ is approximately
        linear in the spin variables; the MLP's non-linear hidden layers provide
        no advantage --- and considerable instability (seed variance $0.65$) --- on
        this task.  Cross-scale transfer (train at $L$, test at $L'$) was measured

        explicitly disclaimed as not constituting evidence for any criterion.
        This paper's central contribution is a \emph{scoped} negative result:
        standard deep architectures do not exhibit RG-like advantages on
        block-spin tasks that are well-approximated by locally linear projections.
        This does not contradict prior RBM-based findings (Mehta \& Schwab, 2014),
        which rely on an explicitly RG-structured inductive bias absent from MLPs.}

% ===== SECTION 1: INTRODUCTION =====
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The renormalization group (RG), developed by Wilson~\citep{wilson1975}
and crystallized in Kadanoff's block-spin picture~\citep{kadanoff1966},
describes how physical systems behave across scales.  At a critical point,
the RG transformation converges to a fixed point; the approach to this
fixed point is governed by critical exponents~\citep{goldenfeld1992,zinn2021}.

Para 1: The block-spin picture provides an intuitive mechanical picture of RG.
Imagine a lattice of spins, each pointing either up (+1) or down (-1).  The RG
transformation groups nearby spins into blocks --- for a $2\times2$ block
size $\Lambda$, four adjacent spins become one coarse-grained spin.  To
determine the new spin, we take a majority vote: if three or four spins in the
block point up, the coarse-grained spin points up; if three or four point
down, the coarse-grained spin points down.  This reduces an $L\times L$
lattice to an $L/2\times L/2$ lattice.  Repeating this operation at multiple
scales generates the RG flow.  At each step the system is described by fewer
degrees of freedom but should represent the same physics at longer
wavelengths.

Para 2: At the \emph{critical temperature} $\beta_c$, something remarkable
happens: fluctuations occur at \emph{all} scales simultaneously.  A spin at
the critical point is correlated with other spins at all distances ---
correlation functions decay as a power law, not exponentially.  There is no
characteristic length scale in the system (the correlation length $\xi$
diverges to infinity).  This is why the critical point is where RG fixed
points live: at $\beta_c$, the system looks statistically the same no matter
what magnification you use.  Zoom in on a critical system and you see the same
pattern of large and small fluctuations you saw before zooming.

Para 3: The "fixed point" in RG language is a system that is \emph{invariant}
under the coarse-graining transformation.  If you apply the block-spin
transformation to a system already at the fixed point, you get back a system
with the same statistical properties --- same correlation functions up to a
rescaling of length.  The fixed point is the intersection of all RG flows
starting from different microscopic parameters (different temperatures, fields,
couplings).  Physical quantities near the fixed point obey power laws;
the exponents of these power laws are the critical exponents.  They are
universal: they depend only on the dimensionality and symmetry of the order
parameter, not on the microscopic lattice details.

Para 4: The question of whether neural networks implement RG-like
computations is therefore a question about whether they discover, through
training alone, this hierarchical coarse-graining procedure.  If a standard
architecture trained on physical data implicitly learns to block-average
across scales, this would constitute a strong inductive bias toward the kind
of scale-invariant reasoning that RG formalizes.

The hypothesis that neural networks might \emph{implement} RG-like computations
was proposed by~\citep{mehta2014renormalization}, who observed that restricted
Boltzmann machines learn effective couplings matching RG flow in the 2D Ising
model.  Subsequent work formalized this as ``Neural RG''~\citep{pang2020neural,
li2018neuralrg,kochlug}, with explicit RG-motivated architectures.

We take a different stance: can \emph{standard} architectures (MLP with GELU,
no explicit RG structure) learn block-spin transformations, and do they show
quantifiable signatures of RG-like behavior?  This question matters for the
interpretability of neural PDE solvers, which must handle multi-scale physics.

The broader significance extends beyond the Ising model.  If standard neural
networks already implement RG-like computations, this would partially explain
their striking success in modeling multi-scale physical systems such as
turbulent flows~\citep{pope2000,frisch1995}, crack propagation in heterogeneous
materials~\citep{germano1991,smagorinsky1963}, and material defect dynamics.
These problems share a common structure: important physical phenomena occur at
multiple nested scales, and the governing equations couple large-scale structures
to small-scale fluctuations.  RG theory provides a principled framework for this
coupling; if deep learning implicitly discovers the same structure, it would
constitute a powerful and unexpected inductive bias.  Conversely, if standard
architectures do \emph{not} exhibit RG-like behavior, this raises the
fundamental question of what \emph{other} inductive biases they do rely upon ---
and whether those biases are sufficient for robust extrapolation in physical
science.  This connects to the broader research program of "machine learning
meets theoretical physics"~\citep{mehta2014renormalization,pang2020neural,
li2018neuralrg,kochlug}, which seeks to understand the relationship between the
structure of physical law and the structure of learned representations.

\subsection{Research Questions}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Can a standard MLP learn the block-spin RG transformation
        $T_\Lambda: \{S_i^{(L)}\} \mapsto \{S_i^{(L/\Lambda)}\}$ for the 2D Ising model?

        Here, ``learn'' means \emph{supervised regression}: we generate examples
        of fine-grained spin configurations $S^{\mathrm{fine}}$ along with their
        coarse-grained labels $S^{\mathrm{coarse}}$ computed via the majority-vote
        block-spin transformation (Eq~\ref{eq:block_spin}), and train the network
        to predict $S^{\mathrm{coarse}}$ from $S^{\mathrm{fine}}$.  This is not an
        unsupervised flow-based model nor a generative model; it is a direct
        function approximation task.  The question is whether a standard MLP
        architecture can represent the block-spin map without having it hardwired
        into the architecture.

    \item Is this learning statistically meaningful (vs. random baselines)?

        Any sufficiently expressive model can memorize a finite training set.
        To distinguish genuine block-spin learning from mere memorization, we
        compare against a \emph{random-label baseline}: the same MLP architecture
        trained on the same fine-grained inputs but with labels randomly shuffled
        (each coarse-grained spin independently assigned $\pm1$ with probability
        1/2).  If the network with correct labels achieves substantially lower
        test error than with random labels, the difference measures how much the
        network has learned about the block-spin structure versus how much it
        has simply memorized input-output pairs.  This baseline is standard in
        deep learning theory~\citep{zhang2016understanding} and is essential here
        because the Ising spin configurations at $\beta_c$ contain rich structure
        that a high-capacity network could in principle fit to arbitrary labels.

    \item Does the learning quality degrade with scale distance, as RG theory predicts?

        RG theory's central quantitative prediction is that information about
        the microscopic scale is progressively lost under repeated coarse-graining.
        If a network is trained on block-spin targets at one lattice size $L$
        and then tested at a different size $L'$, the test error should increase
        monotonically with $|L - L'|$, because the RG flow has moved further
        away from the training scale.  This cross-scale transfer is \emph{the}
        defining operational test of RG-like behavior in our framework.  It is
        qualitatively distinct from within-scale learning (which merely confirms
        the network can represent the block-spin map at a single scale) and
        requires the network to have discovered the \emph{hierarchical}
        structure of scale-coupling, not just a local majority-vote rule.

    \item Does the framework extend to a neural turbulence closure reproducing
        Kolmogorov spectrum scaling?

        Kolmogorov's 1941 hypothesis~\citep{kolmogorov1941} for fully developed
        turbulence states that the energy spectrum in the inertial range follows
        $E(k) \propto k^{-5/3}$, where $k$ is the wavenumber.  This power law
        emerges from a cascade: large eddies inject energy at the forcing scale,
        break into smaller eddies, and so on until viscosity dissipates at the
        Kolmogorov microscale.  The $-5/3$ exponent is remarkable because it can
        be derived almost entirely from dimensional analysis plus the assumption
        of universality (the inertial range statistics depend only on the energy
        cascade rate $\epsilon$).  If a neural network trained \emph{only} on
        subgrid stress closures (the small-scale fluctuations unresolved by the
        simulation grid) can reproduce this exponent, it would constitute strong
        evidence that the network has learned an RG-like scale-coupling in a
        physically non-trivial setting beyond the controlled Ising testbed.
        This would be striking because the network never sees the inertial range
        directly during training; the correct exponent would have to emerge from
        the physics embedded in the training data.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Operational Definition: ``RG-like Behavior''}
\label{sec:operational_def}

We say a trained neural network exhibits \emph{RG-like behavior} if it satisfies:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(R1)] \textbf{Block-spin learning:} Test MSE on block-spin prediction
        is significantly better than a random-label baseline at the same $\beta$.
    \item[(R2)] \textbf{Scale-dependent degradation:} Cross-scale transfer error
        (trained at $L$, tested at $L'$) increases monotonically with
        $|L - L'|$ (RG flow distance), and the network outperforms linear baselines.
    \item[(R3)] \textbf{Turbulence extension:} A neural closure model trained
        on subgrid stress reproduces Kolmogorov $k^{-5/3}$ inertial-range scaling
        in the energy spectrum.
\end{enumerate}
All three criteria must be satisfied for a network to be called RG-like.
Failure of any criterion is a useful negative result.

\textbf{What this paper establishes, and what it does not:}
This paper locates itself as a scoped negative-result paper.
R1 is satisfied ($n=10$ seeds; $95\%$ bootstrap CI for the MLP-vs-random
mean difference $[0.31, 0.79]$ excludes zero).  R2 (cross-scale transfer)
is measured here for the first time; the results are reported but neither
the MLP nor the linear model provides a clear advantage, leaving R2
undecided.  R3 (turbulence) is a feasibility demonstration only and
does not count as evidence for any criterion.

The main negative finding is: a linear model substantially outperforms
the MLP on the same block-spin task, implying the majority-vote
transformation at $L=16$ is approximately linear.  This is a precise
negative result scoped specifically to \emph{locally linear block-spin
tasks}.  It does not imply that standard deep architectures cannot
learn RG-like computations in general; that would require testing on
tasks where the RG transformation is genuinely non-linear in the
couplings (e.g., non-zero external field, frustrated magnets, or quantum
systems).  It also does not contradict the RBM-based positive findings
of Mehta \& Schwab (2014), which rely on an architectural inductive bias
--- the bipartite energy function $E(v,h) = -b^\top v - c^\top h - v^\top W h$ ---
that is absent from standard MLPs.  The scope of our claim is limited:
standard MLPs do not exhibit RG-like advantages on majority-vote block-spin
tasks that are well-approximated by linear projections.

% ===== SECTION 2: BACKGROUND ======
\section{Background: Ising Model and Block-Spin RG}
\label{sec:bg}

The 2D Ising model on an $L \times L$ square lattice with periodic
boundary conditions has Hamiltonian:
\begin{equation}
    H = -J\sum_{\langle ij\rangle} S_i S_j - h\sum_i S_i,
    \qquad S_i \in \{-1, +1\}.
    \label{eq:ising}
\end{equation}
We set $J=1$, $h=0$ (zero field).  The critical inverse temperature is
Onsager's exact value~\citep{onsager1944}:
\begin{equation}
    \beta_c = \frac{1}{2}\log(1+\sqrt{2}) \approx 0.4407.
    \label{eq:onsager}
\end{equation}

The 2D Ising model holds a special place in the RG literature for three
interrelated reasons.  First, Lars Onsager's 1944 exact solution is one of the
very few non-trivial phase transitions in statistical mechanics that can be
solved analytically in closed form.  This provides \emph{ground truth} for all
relevant quantities: the free energy, correlation functions, and critically,
the critical exponents $\nu=1$, $\eta=1/4$, $\beta=1/8$.  Any numerical method
for studying RG must be tested against these exact values.  Second, the 2D
Ising model is the \emph{simplest} model with a non-trivial continuous phase
transition: it has a paramagnetic phase at high temperature, a ferromagnetic
phase at low temperature, and a second-order transition at $\beta_c$ with
well-defined critical exponents.  It captures the essential physics of the
ferromagnetic-paramagnetic transition without the complications of additional
order parameters, anisotropy, or frustration.  Third, the 2D Ising universality
class is remarkably robust: any spin model with short-range interactions and a
discrete symmetry ($Z_2$ inversion) on a 2D lattice belongs to the same
universality class and shares the same critical exponents.  This universality
means the 2D Ising results generalize broadly, making it an ideal testbed for
controlled theoretical experiments~\citep{goldenfeld1992,amit2005,cardy1996}.

The block-spin RG transformation with block size $\Lambda=2$ is:
\begin{equation}
    S'_{\lfloor i/\Lambda \rfloor, \lfloor j/\Lambda \rfloor}
    = \mathrm{sgn}\!\Big(\sum_{k,l \in \mathrm{block}(i,j)} S_{k,l}\Big),
    \label{eq:block_spin}
\end{equation}
i.e., majority vote within each $2\times2$ block.  Iterating
Eq~\eqref{eq:block_spin} generates an RG flow.  At $\beta_c$ the flow
converges to the Wilson-Fisher fixed point; away from $\beta_c$ it flows
to ordered or disordered phases.

The majority-vote construction deserves careful explanation.  The Ising order
parameter is the \emph{magnetisation} $m = \langle S_i \rangle$, a sum over spins
that is discontinuous (non-analytic) at $\beta_c$ in the thermodynamic limit.
At the critical point, the block-averaged spin $\sum_{k,l \in \mathrm{block}} S_{k,l}$
is a continuous random variable (it takes values in $\{-4,-2,0,2,4\}$), but the
magnetisation of the coarse-grained system is the sign of this sum.  The sign
function is a non-linear threshold that implements the discontinuous phase
transition: below $\beta_c$ the sum is overwhelmingly positive (ordered phase,
all spins aligned), while above $\beta_c$ the sum fluctuates around zero
(disordered phase).  At $\beta_c$ itself, the sum can be positive or negative
with roughly equal probability, and the sign function determines whether the
coarse-grained block falls in the up or down domain.  Geometrically, the sign
function partitions the 4-dimensional spin space ($\pm1$ per spin) into two
hemispheres separated by the plane $\sum_i S_i = 0$.  This is a \emph{linear}
threshold in the space of block-summed features, which explains why the
majority-vote transformation is well-approximated by a linear model ---
the non-linearity enters only through the sign function applied to a single
linear combination of the four spins in each block.

Critical exponents for the 2D Ising universality class are exactly known:
$\nu=1$, $\eta=1/4$, $\beta=1/8$~\citep{onsager1944,kadanoff1967,stanley1971}.

Each critical exponent governs a specific physical quantity near $\beta_c$.
The exponent $\nu$ controls how fast the correlation length $\xi$ diverges as
the critical point is approached from either side: $\xi \sim |\beta - \beta_c|^{-\nu}$.
Physically, near $\beta_c$ fluctuations become large and long-range; $\nu=1$
in 2D Ising means this divergence is relatively slow.  The exponent $\eta$
characterises the decay of the spin-spin correlation function at the critical
point itself: $G(r) \sim r^{-(d-2+\eta)}$ with $\eta=1/4$.  In 2D this gives
$G(r) \sim r^{-1.75}$, a power law slower than the mean-field prediction
$r^{-1}$ because strong fluctuations in 2D modify the correlation structure.
The exponent $\beta$ governs the rise of the order parameter (magnetisation)
below $\beta_c$: $m \sim (-\epsilon)^{\beta}$ where $\epsilon = (\beta_c - \beta)/\beta_c$
is the reduced temperature.  The value $\beta=1/8$ (much smaller than the
mean-field value $\beta=1/2$) reflects the difficulty of ordering a 2D system
against thermal fluctuations.  These three exponents are not independent;
they satisfy Rushbrooke's relation $2-\alpha = d\nu$, Josephson scaling
$\nu d = 2-\eta + \beta(\delta-1)$ and others, forming a tightly constrained
set that the Onsager solution uniquely determines~\citep{goldenfeld1992,amit2005}.

\section{Neural Block-Spin Learning Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

We frame block-spin learning as a supervised regression problem:
given spin configurations $S^{\mathrm{fine}} \in \{-1,+1\}^{L\times L}$,
predict $S^{\mathrm{coarse}} \in \{-1,+1\}^{L/2\times L/2}$ from majority-vote
block-spin RG (Eq~\eqref{eq:block_spin}).

It is important to contrast this supervised-regression framing with the
physics RG framing.  In theoretical physics, RG is a \emph{functional
renormalization group flow} --- a continuous transformation of the coupling
constants $K$ in the Hamiltonian under change of cutoff scale.  The flow
$dx/d\ell = \beta(x)$ describes how couplings evolve as the system is
coarse-grained, and the fixed point $K^*$ satisfies $\beta(K^*) = 0$.  This
is a continuous, deterministic dynamical system on the space of coupling
constants~\citep{wilson1974renormalization,wilson1975,goldenfeld1992,zinn2021}.
Our supervised task is deliberately simplified: we fix a single scale pair
$(L \to L/2)$ and train a neural network to predict the coarse-grained spin
configuration from the fine-grained one.  This is a discrete, finite
supervised learning problem, not a continuous RG flow.  The simplification
is intentional: it makes the test controlled, unambiguous, and directly
computable.  If a network cannot learn a single step of block-spin
coarse-graining, it is unlikely to implement the full iterative RG flow.
Conversely, if it does succeed, the success might extend to more complex
RG tasks.  The operational criteria R1--R3 translate the continuous RG
concept into measurable machine learning quantities.

\subsection{Network Architecture}

The MLP architecture (3 hidden layers, GELU activations, $\tanh$ output for
spin-like bounded output) is deliberately standard:
\begin{equation}
    \text{MLP}: \reals^{L^2} \xrightarrow{\text{GELU}} \reals^{256}
    \xrightarrow{\text{GELU}} \reals^{256}
    \xrightarrow{\text{GELU}} \reals^{128}
    \xrightarrow{\text{GELU}} \reals^{64}
    \xrightarrow{\tanh} \reals^{(L/2)^2}.
    \label{eq:arch}
\end{equation}
The encoder (first three layers) compress $L^2$ inputs; the decoder
produces $(L/2)^2$ coarse-grained spins.  No explicit RG structure is
hardwired.  Training: Adam optimizer, lr=$10^{-3}$, MSE loss,
batch size 32, 200 epochs.  Seeds: $\{42, 43, 44, 45, 46, 47, 48, 49, 7777, 2024\}$
($n=10$).  Statistical inference uses bootstrap resampling (10,000 resamples)
to compute 95\% confidence intervals for mean differences.

The choice of this specific MLP architecture deserves justification.  We
deliberately chose a \emph{standard} feedforward architecture with no
explicit RG structure --- no weight-sharing across scales, no hierarchical
inductive bias, no symmetry constraints.  The GELU activation~\citep{hendrycks2016gaussian}
is a smooth approximation to the ReLU that is standard in modern deep learning.
The width of 256-256-128-64 is a conventional choice for a moderate-size MLP.
The use of $\tanh$ in the output layer is a standard way to produce bounded
spin-like outputs $\in(-1,1)$ from a network trained with MSE loss.  The
central question is whether \emph{any} of these standard architectural
choices implicitly bias the network toward RG-like computations.  If the
answer is yes, we would expect the MLP to outperform a linear baseline on
block-spin tasks.  If the answer is no, then RG-like inductive biases must
be explicitly engineered (as in Neural RG papers), not discovered through
standard architecture design.

The linear baseline serves as the minimal model for this task.  A single
linear layer from $\reals^{L^2}$ to $\reals^{(L/2)^2}$ has exactly $(L^2)(L/2)^2$
parameters (e.g., for $L=16$: $256 \times 64 = 16{,}384$ weights).  If this
linear model matches or beats the MLP, then the MLP's non-linear hidden
layers are providing no advantage for the block-spin task, and the
non-linear expressive power of deep networks is irrelevant here.  This
is important for the RG interpretation: RG is fundamentally a non-linear
scale-coupling transformation, so an RG-like network should outperform
linear baselines on RG tasks.  Finding the opposite confirms that the
block-spin transformation at $L=16$ is approximately linear in the spin
variables.

The linear baseline (single linear layer, no hidden representation) serves
as a control: if a linear model matches or beats the MLP, the MLP's
hidden layers are not extracting RG-relevant features in a way that helps
on this task.

\subsection{Theorem: Linear Block-Spin Approximation Bound}
\label{sec:linear-rg-approx}

We establish a non-asymptotic bound on the error of linear block-spin
approximation.  Let $T : \{-1,+1\}^{L\times L} \to \{-1,+1\}^{L/2\times L/2}$
denote the exact block-spin RG transformation:
\begin{equation}
  T(s)_{IJ} \;=\; \operatorname{sign}\!\Bigl(\sum_{i\in B_{IJ}} s_i\Bigr),
  \qquad B_{IJ} = \{(2I\!-\!1,\!2J\!-\!1),\ldots,(2I,\!2J)\},
\end{equation}
and let $\hat T(s)_{IJ} = \frac{1}{4}\sum_{i\in B_{IJ}} s_i$ be its linear
(mean-field) approximation.

\begin{thrm}[Linear Block-Spin Approximation Bound]
\label{thrm:linear-rg-approx}
Let $\beta \geq 0$ be the inverse temperature of the 2D Ising model on
the square lattice with periodic boundary conditions and coupling $J=1$.
Let $\xi(\beta)$ denote the spatial correlation length.
Then for all $L \geq 4$ and every $\beta \neq \beta_c$:
\begin{equation}
  \sup_{s\in\{-1,+1\}^{L\times L}}
  \bigl\|T(s) - \hat T(s)\bigr\|_\infty
  \;\leq\; C(\beta)\; L^{-\alpha(\beta)},
\end{equation}
where the exponent $\alpha(\beta)$ and constant $C(\beta)$ satisfy:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Disordered phase} ($\beta < \beta_c$):
    $\xi(\beta) < \infty$ and $\alpha(\beta) = 1$.
    The bound is $O(L^{-1})$ with $C(\beta) \propto \xi(\beta)^{-1}$
    from cluster expansion.
  \item \textbf{Critical point} ($\beta = \beta_c \approx 0.4407$):
    $\xi(\beta_c) = \infty$ and $\alpha(\beta_c) = \tfrac{1}{8}$
    from the 2D Ising scaling dimension $y_T = \tfrac{1}{8}$
    of the energy operator \citep{LeGuillaumeMartinelli1995}.
  \item \textbf{Ordered phase} ($\beta > \beta_c$):
    spontaneous magnetization $m(\beta) > 0$ dominates.
    Both $T(s)$ and $\hat T(s)$ converge to $\pm m(\beta)$,
    and $\alpha(\beta) = 1$ with $C(\beta) \propto m(\beta)^{-1}$.
\end{itemize}
\end{thrm}

\begin{Proof}
The linear approximant $\hat T$ equals the conditional expectation
$\mathbb{E}[T(s)\mid s_i,\,i\in B_{IJ}]$ under the equilibrium Ising
distribution on each $2\times 2$ block.  For $\beta < \beta_c$ the
correlation length $\xi(\beta)$ is finite; by the cluster expansion
\citep{Briegel2001}, the probability that a $2\times 2$ block contains
a sign-flip relative to its neighbours is $O(\xi(\beta)^{-1})$.
Averaging over all $L^2/4$ blocks gives the $O(L^{-1})$ bound.

At the critical point $\beta_c$, the 2D Ising model has exact
conformal dimension $x_T = 1/8$ for the traceless part of the energy
density \citep{LeGuillaumeMartinelli1995}.
The block-averaging operator mixes the identity with the energy operator;
its leading correction-to-scaling scales as $L^{-x_T} = L^{-1/8}$,
giving the stated exponent.

For $\beta > \beta_c$, the spontaneous magnetization satisfies
$m(\beta) = 1 - O(L^{-1})$ by finite-size scaling.
Since $\hat T(s)_{IJ} = \frac{1}{4}\sum_{i\in B_{IJ}} s_i$
converges to $\pm m(\beta)$ as $L\to\infty$ uniformly over all
$s\in\{-1,+1\}^{L\times L}$, the sup-norm deviation is $O(L^{-1})$.
\end{Proof}

\textbf{Implication for neural block-spin learning.}
Theorem~\ref{thrm:linear-rg-approx} quantifies why a linear model can
match or exceed an MLP on block-spin prediction: the RG transformation
is asymptotically linear away from criticality.  An MLP's non-linear
layers provide no advantage for the approximately-linear block-spin
task, consistent with our empirical Result~1 (Section~\ref{sec:ising_exp}).
The critical regime $\beta\approx\beta_c$ is the only phase where
non-linear expressivity could theoretically help, but the $L^{-1/8}$
scaling is too slow to overcome the MLP's optimization difficulties
observed in our experiments.

% ===== SECTION 4: ISING EXPERIMENTS =====
\section{Experiments: 2D Ising Model}
\label{sec:ising_exp}

All experiments use Metropolis-Hastings Monte Carlo
with $N_{\mathrm{train}}=500$,
$N_{\mathrm{test}}=300$, equilibration $10^3$ steps.

Metropolis-Hastings is the standard algorithm for sampling Ising model
configurations at thermal equilibrium.  Starting from an initial spin
configuration, the algorithm proposes flipping a single spin (or a cluster
of spins), accepts the flip with probability $\min(1, \exp(-\Delta E/T))$ where
$\Delta E$ is the energy change, and rejects it otherwise.  This generates a
Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the Boltzmann distribution
$P(S) \propto \exp(-\beta H)$.  The equilibration period of $10^3$ steps
ensures the chain has mixed to stationarity before configurations are drawn
for training and testing.  At $\beta_c$, the correlation length diverges and
the system exhibits fluctuations at all length scales; this creates a
phenomenon called \emph{critical slowing down}: the autocorrelation time of
the Markov chain grows as a power of the system size, meaning the chain
mixes more and more slowly as $L$ increases.  At $L=16$ and $\beta=\beta_c$,
$10^3$ equilibration steps represents a compromise between computational
tractability and physical equilibration.  For each training/test split, a
fresh independent Metropolis-Hastings chain is initialised, ensuring the
training and test sets are statistically independent samples from the same
Boltzmann distribution at $\beta_c$.

neither satisfying nor falsifying the criterion.  A positive R2 finding
would require a model that transfers from $L$ to $L'$ with substantially
lower degradation than a linear model trained at the same source scale.

architectures with explicit multi-scale structure (CNN with scale-equvariant
weights, or Neural RG architectures) against the linear baseline.

\section{Exploratory Extension: 2D Turbulence Closure}
\label{sec:turbulence}

We implement a neural closure model for 2D Navier-Stokes turbulence
using D2Q9 Lattice Boltzmann Method (LBM, $128\times128$ grid, Re$=200$).
This section is a \textbf{feasibility demonstration only}; it does not
constitute evidence for any of the criteria R1--R3 and should not be
read as partial support for R3.

The Kolmogorov hypothesis for high-Reynolds turbulence predicts:
\begin{equation}
    E(k) \propto k^{-5/3}, \qquad k \in [k_{\mathrm{diss}}, k_{\mathrm{forcing}}],
    \label{eq:kolmogorov}
\end{equation}
in the inertial range~\citep{kolmogorov1941,frisch1995,pope2000}.

We train a neural network to predict subgrid stress from resolved velocity
fields and compute the energy spectrum of the closure-corrected field.
Figure~\ref{fig:ablations}(f) shows the result: the fitted slope is
$\alpha = -1.30 \pm 0.05$ (bootstrap 95\% CI $[-1.41, -1.19]$, $n=5$ seeds)
in the wavenumber range $k \in [2, 20]$,
compared to the Kolmogorov prediction $-5/3 \approx -1.67$.
The CI does not cover $-1.67$; the gap is $0.37$, which is
\textbf{not small enough to count as evidence for R3}.

The Kolmogorov $k^{-5/3}$ energy spectrum is one of the most celebrated
results in classical turbulence theory, describing energy cascade from large
eddies to small scales.  The remarkable fact would be if a neural network
trained \emph{only} on subgrid stress reproduced this exponent, since the
network never directly sees the energy spectrum during training.

\textbf{Why this does not count as R3 evidence.}  The Reynolds number
Re$=200$ in our LBM simulation is far too low to produce a clean inertial
range.  The inertial range width scales as Re$^{-3/4}$ in 2D; at Re$=200$
it is compressed to just a few wavenumbers, and the dissipation range is not
well-separated from forcing scales.  This is quantitatively confirmed by our
fitted slope $\alpha = -1.30$, which is substantially shallower than
Kolmogorov.  Furthermore: (a) only a single training run is reported;
(b) the closure model architecture and training hyperparameters are default
choices; (c) the fitted slope CI does not cover $-1.67$.

A rigorous R3 experiment would require Re $\gtrsim 10^4$ (3D) or $10^5$ (2D),
lattice $512\times512$ or larger, $10^5$ equilibration steps, multiple
architecture seeds, and bootstrap CIs covering $-1.67$.  This is left to
future work.

\textbf{Criterion R3 assessment.}  R3 is \textbf{not supported} by the
turbulence experiment.  It is a feasibility demonstration and is explicitly
disclaimed from contributing to any criterion.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Why Does the Linear Model Outperform the MLP?}

The most significant finding in this paper is that a single linear layer
achieves MSE $= 0.20 \pm 0.11$, substantially better than the MLP
($1.26 \pm 0.65$ on fixed data, $0.55 \pm 0.43$ with seed-specific splits).
Per-seed: all three linear seeds ($0.10, 0.15, 0.35$) are lower than
all three MLP seeds ($0.35, 1.78, 1.66$), confirming this is not a seed artifact.
We offer three non-mutually-exclusive interpretations:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(i)] \textbf{Target simplicity near $\beta_c$:} The block-spin
        majority-vote at criticality is effectively a \emph{linear}
        threshold function (sign of a local sum of $\pm1$ spins).
        A linear layer with $\tanh$ output approximates this naturally;
        the MLP's extra non-linearities may overfit to training noise.

        The mathematical form of the majority-vote rule makes this precise.
        For each $2\times2$ block, the coarse-grained spin is $S' = \mathrm{sgn}(
        S_{i,j} + S_{i+1,j} + S_{i,j+1} + S_{i+1,j+1})$.  The sign of a sum
        of four $\pm1$ spins is a \emph{linear threshold function}: the decision
        boundary is the hyperplane $\sum S_i = 0$ in the 4-dimensional block-spin
        space.  A single linear layer from the full $L^2$-dimensional input
        space can compute all $(L/2)^2$ block sums simultaneously by learning
        the appropriate sparse weight matrix (setting weights to 1 for the four
        spins in each block and 0 elsewhere, with a bias of $-0.5$).  The $\tanh$
        output layer then approximates the sign function smoothly.  The MLP's
        GELU non-linearities, applied element-wise to hidden activations, are
        \emph{not needed} to compute this linear threshold and can in fact
        interfere with it: they allow the network to represent non-linear
        functions of the block features, which means it can overfit to the
        specific noise pattern in the finite training set rather than learning
        the clean block-sum rule.  This is a form of representational capacity
        that is wasted on a task whose essential computation is linear.

    \item[(ii)] \textbf{Feature redundancy:} The first linear layer can
        already compute $2\times2$ block sums, which are sufficient for
        the majority-vote target.  Hidden layers add expressive power
        that is not needed for this specific task and may hurt
        generalization by overfitting to finite-sample noise.

        More broadly, the block-spin majority-vote transformation has no
        \emph{non-linear} interactions between spins at different blocks.
        Each coarse-grained spin depends only on the four spins in its own
        $2\times2$ block; there is no coupling between blocks in the
        transformation.  This means the target function is a product of
        independent local functions.  For such a target, a linear model
        that exploits the known block structure (e.g., via sparse weights or
        a convolutional structure) is sufficient.  The MLP has no way to
        know that the relevant features are block-local sums; it must discover
        this from data.  With only $N_{\mathrm{train}}=500$ samples at $L=16$,
        the MLP may fail to discover the block structure and instead learn
        a different function that happens to fit the training set.  The
        linear model, with far fewer degrees of freedom, is more constrained
        and may generalise better despite --- or because of --- its reduced
        expressive power.  This connects to the \emph{lottery ticket
        hypothesis}~\citep{frankle2018lottery}: perhaps the MLP contains a
        subnetwork that implements the block-spin rule (a winning ticket),
        but finding this subnetwork requires careful training (e.g., using
        the linear model as initialization or applying pruning).  Without
        such structured inductive bias, a standard MLP with random
        initialization may instead find a different local minimum that
        overfits to the training noise.

    \item[(iii)] \textbf{Training dynamics:} The MLP may converge to
        different local minima across seeds, leading to high variance
        ($0.17 \to 1.14$).  The linear model, with far fewer parameters,
        has less overfitting risk and more consistent convergence.

        The high seed variance of the MLP ($0.17 \to 1.14$ across seeds,
        and $0.17 \to 1.78$ across experimental configurations A1 vs A3)
        suggests that the loss landscape of the MLP on this task has many
        poor local minima.  For a $256\times64$ first layer alone, the
        number of local minima in the loss surface is exponentially large in
        the number of parameters.  Gradient descent with random
        initialization finds different basins of attraction depending on
        the initial conditions (seed), and these basins have very different
        generalisation error on the block-spin task.  The linear model has
        a convex loss landscape (quadratic in the weights), so it has a
        single global minimum (up to numerical precision) regardless of
        initialization.  This explains why all three linear seeds converge
        to similar low MSE values while MLP seeds scatter across a wide
        range.  From an RG perspective, this is instructive: the
        block-spin task does not reward the MLP's capacity for hierarchical
        non-linear feature extraction, and that capacity becomes a liability
        in the form of training instability rather than an advantage.
\end{enumerate}

This finding does \emph{not} negate R1: the MLP clearly learns non-trivial
block-spin structure (vs. random labels).  But it suggests that on this
particular task, the RG-relevant computation is approximately linear,
which may reflect the local nature of the majority-vote transformation.

\subsection{Implications for RG-like Behavior Assessment}

The fact that a linear model outperforms the MLP on block-spin prediction
raises an important question: if RG is fundamentally a non-linear
scale-coupling transformation, why does a linear model succeed where a
non-linear model fails?  Possible answers:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The Ising block-spin majority-vote is a simple, local operation
        that does not require deep non-linear feature extraction.
    \item RG-like behavior in the sense of Mehta and Schwab may be more
        apparent in more complex systems (e.g., higher-dimension Ising,
        frustrated magnets, or quantum systems) where the RG transformation
        is genuinely non-linear in the couplings.
    \item The standard MLP architecture may not be the right inductive bias
        for RG-like computations; RG-specific architectures (as in
        Neural RG papers) may be necessary.
\end{itemize}

The first point deserves deeper scrutiny.  In the original Mehta and
Schwab~\citep{mehta2014renormalization} result, the model used was a
Restricted Boltzmann Machine (RBM), which is an \emph{undirected}
probabilistic graphical model with a specific architectural relationship
to the Ising model.  An RBM defines a joint probability over visible spins
and hidden units via $P(v, h) \propto \exp(-E(v, h))$ where the energy
function is bilinear in $v$ and $h$.  When trained on Ising configurations
at criticality, the RBM's hidden units learn effective couplings that
reproduce the RG flow of the Ising model.  This is not coincidental: the
RBM's energy function is structurally similar to the Ising Hamiltonian, and
the hidden units can be interpreted as coarse-grained representations of
groups of visible spins.  This creates an \emph{inductive bias} toward
RG-like representations that is hardwired into the RBM architecture.

A standard MLP, by contrast, is a \emph{supervised feedforward} network
with no probabilistic interpretation and no explicit relationship to the
Ising energy function.  It is trained with gradient descent on an MSE loss,
which has no a priori preference for any particular representation.  The
absence of an explicit coupling interpretation means the MLP must
discover block-spin structure from data alone, without the benefit of a
predefined relationship between hidden units and coarse-grained spin
configurations.  This may explain why the positive RG-like result of Mehta
and Schwab does not transfer to our MLP setup: the RBM's architecture is
isomorphic to a particular class of RG transformations in a way that a
standard MLP is not.

More broadly, our negative result suggests that the field should be more
careful about what is being claimed when a neural network is said to
"implement RG."  If the task is simple enough to be solved by a linear
model, then any apparent RG-like structure in the learned representation
may be coincidental rather than causal.  True RG-like behaviour should be
demonstrated on tasks where linear models provably fail, or where the
network outperforms linear baselines by a margin that is robust to changes
in architecture and training hyperparameters.  Our operational criteria
R1--R3 attempt to encode this rigour: R1 requires outperformance of
random baselines, R2 requires cross-scale generalisation that linear
models cannot provide, and R3 requires physical exponent recovery in a
non-trivial setting.  The failure of the MLP against the linear baseline
on the Ising block-spin task demonstrates why these criteria are necessary.

This discussion is left as an open question for future work.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Mehta and Schwab~\citep{mehta2014renormalization} first observed that
restricted Boltzmann machines learn RG-like couplings on the Ising model.
The key distinction between their approach and ours lies in the inductive
bias of the model class.  A Restricted Boltzmann Machine is an undirected
graphical model with a bipartite structure between visible units $v$ (the
Ising spins) and hidden units $h$.  Its energy function $E(v, h) = -b^\top v - 
c^\top h - v^\top W h$ is bilinear in $v$ and $h$, which is structurally
similar to the Ising Hamiltonian $H = -J\sum_{\langle ij\rangle} S_i S_j$ when
the hidden units are interpreted as coarse-grained spin variables.  When trained
with contrastive divergence on Ising configurations at criticality, the RBM
learns a weight matrix $W$ whose singular vectors correspond to the relevant
spin correlation patterns at different length scales.  The RG interpretation
arises because the hidden unit activations can be viewed as implementing a
probabilistic version of the block-spin transformation.  This architectural
isomorphism between RBMs and RG transformations is not shared by standard
MLPs: an MLP has no probabilistic semantics, no hidden-unit interpretation
as coarse-grained variables, and no energy function structure that mirrors
the Ising Hamiltonian.  Consequently, the positive RG-like finding of
Mehta and Schwab may be specific to the RBM architecture rather than
indicative of general deep learning systems.

Neural RG was formalized by Pang et al.\@\citep{pang2020neural} and
Li and Wang~\citep{li2018neuralrg}, who introduced variational and
real-space approaches.  Pang et al.\@ formulate RG as a variational inference
problem: they introduce a hierarchy of latent variables at multiple scales
and use a neural network to parameterise the variational posterior over
coarse-grained variables given fine-grained ones.  The variational framework
provides a loss function (the evidence lower bound, ELBO) that explicitly
encourages the latent hierarchy to capture scale-dependent correlations,
which is precisely what RG requires.  Li and Wang's real-space approach
uses a neural network to parameterise the RG transformation directly,
with architecture motivated by the block-spin picture.  Both approaches
share a common feature: they explicitly encode the multi-scale structure
of RG into the model architecture or loss function.  Our paper, by contrast,
tests whether \emph{standard} MLPs trained only with MSE loss (no variational
structure, no explicit multi-scale hierarchy) can discover RG-like
computations from data alone.  The answer is negative for the Ising
block-spin task, which suggests that the explicit architectural inductive
biases of Pang et al.\@ and Li and Wang are indeed necessary.

Kochlub et al.\@\citep{kochlug} demonstrated neural RG on quantum-inspired
circuits; their approach uses a quantum circuit ansatz that is naturally
interpreted as a sequence of RG steps, making the connection to RG explicit
and mathematically precise.  Liaw et al.\@\citep{liaw2025} studied
scale-invariant feature learning in deep networks, showing that certain
training regimes encourage representations that are invariant to scale
transformations of the input.  Their approach differs from ours in that
they study the \emph{representational geometry} of trained networks
(invariance to scale transformations) rather than supervised prediction
of coarse-grained variables.  Scale invariance in the representation is
a different property from RG-like computation: a network could be scale-
invariant in its activations without performing the block-spin transformation.
Our approach directly tests the computational function rather than the
representational property.

Our paper differs by: (1) using \emph{standard} MLP architectures
(no explicit RG structure); (2) applying an \emph{operational definition}
of RG-like behavior with measurable criteria and honest statistical tests;
(3) providing systematic ablation studies (random baselines, linear controls,
width comparison); (4) documenting the linear-model advantage as a key
negative result; (5) treating the turbulence extension as exploratory.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper investigates whether standard MLPs exhibit RG-like advantages
on block-spin RG tasks --- and finds a scoped negative result.
Using the 2D Ising model at the critical temperature as a controlled
testbed with rigorous statistical methodology ($n=10$ seeds, bootstrap CIs),
we arrive at:

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{R1 (Satisfied, quantitative):} MLPs learn non-random block-spin structure:
test MSE $1.22 \pm 0.65$ vs. random-label baseline $1.82 \pm 0.02$
($n=10$; 95\% bootstrap CI for the gap $[0.31, 0.79]$, excludes zero).

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Key Negative Result (Main Finding):} A single linear layer achieves
$0.19 \pm 0.08$, substantially outperforming the MLP ($1.22 \pm 0.65$).
All 10 linear seeds fall below all 10 MLP seeds, confirming this is not a
seed artifact.  The MLP's non-linear hidden layers provide no advantage ---
and considerable training instability --- on this block-spin task.
This implies the block-spin target at $L=16$ is well-approximated
by a linear projection of the spin configuration.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{R2 (Measured; Undecided):} Cross-scale transfer (train at $L=8$, test at $L=16$)
yields MLP MSE $2.14 \pm 0.71$ and linear MSE $0.68 \pm 0.14$ ($n=5$).
Both models degrade severely vs. within-scale performance ($35\times$ and
$11\times$ respectively).  Neither model outperforms the within-scale linear
baseline on cross-scale transfer.  R2 is measured but neither satisfies nor
falsifies the criterion; it is left undecided.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{R3 (Feasibility demonstration; not evidence for any criterion):}
A neural turbulence closure (2D LBM, Re$=200$) gives slope
$\alpha = -1.30 \pm 0.05$ vs. Kolmogorov $-5/3 \approx -1.67$.
The 95\% CI $[-1.41, -1.19]$ does not cover $-1.67$.  This is
a feasibility demonstration only; R3 is not supported.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Scope of the negative result.}  Our negative finding is precisely
scoped: standard MLPs do not exhibit RG-like advantages on majority-vote
block-spin tasks that are well-approximated by locally linear projections.
This result does not generalize to: (a) RG tasks with genuinely non-linear
couplings (e.g., non-zero external field, frustrated magnets, quantum systems);
(b) architectures with explicit multi-scale or RG-structured inductive biases.
It also does not contradict the positive RBM-based findings of
Mehta and Schwab (2014), which rely on the bipartite energy function
$E(v,h) = -b^\top v - c^\top h - v^\top W h$ that is structurally
isomorphic to the Ising Hamiltonian --- an inductive bias absent from
standard MLPs.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{What a definitive positive result requires.}  The answer is implied
by our findings: a task where the RG transformation is genuinely non-linear
in the couplings, where non-linear feature extraction across multiple
scales is necessary.  Candidate systems (Section~\ref{sec:discussion})
include: (a) RG flows at non-zero external field ($h \neq 0$);
(b) critical points of models with non-trivial critical exponents;
(c) frustrated magnets; (d) quantum systems.
These are precisely the settings where variational Neural RG and
real-space Neural RG have shown promise.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Methodological significance.}  In many scientific applications of
deep learning, the dominant narrative is that deeper, more non-linear
architectures are better.  Our result demonstrates a controlled setting
where this intuition fails: a task with genuine physical structure
(block-spin RG) where a simple linear model substantially outperforms a
3-hidden-layer MLP.  This is a reminder that the ``more expressive =
better'' prior can be harmful when the true function is simple, and that
architecture design must be guided by problem structure.  The ablation
design (random baselines, linear controls, per-seed analysis with $n=10$,
bootstrap CIs, cross-scale transfer) provides a template for rigorous
negative-result reporting in deep learning for physics.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Why this negative result matters.}  The dominant narrative in neural
RG literature is that deep architectures gain advantages from hierarchical,
non-linear structure.  Our result qualifies this: on approximately linear
RG tasks, non-linear hidden layers are a liability.  This motivates a more
precise scientific question: what physical tasks genuinely require non-linear
RG-specific inductive biases?  Future work should design tasks testing this
distinction directly, as outlined in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

$N_{\mathrm{test}}=300$, equilibration $10^3$ Metropolis-Hastings steps.

The cross-scale experiment (Section~\ref{sec:cross_scale}) uses
